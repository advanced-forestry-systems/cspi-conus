# Social media posts — CSPI v2.0.0 release

*Revised 14 June 2026 after v0.10b correction: SICOND orthogonality claim was a base-age Simpson paradox; SITECLCD finding is the robust headline.*

## LinkedIn variant A (long-form, technical audience: forest biometricians, growth-and-yield modelers, federal agency staff)

For nearly a century, U.S. foresters have used site index as the standard way to rank stand productivity. New CRSF research across 66,000 plus FIA plots covering the conterminous United States: a random forest predicting the Forest Service's own seven-class site productivity classification (SITECLCD) from biomass growth increment alone reaches OOB R squared of 0.808; the same model from site index alone reaches only 0.751. A six percentage point gap.

We computed four productivity measures at each plot: a unified-target site index, biomass growth increment, asymptotic biomass, and MODIS net primary production. Mean site index is essentially flat across the FIA's seven productivity classes (30.6 to 29.6 meters). Mean biomass growth drops from 2.11 to 0.92 megagrams per hectare per year across the same classes, a factor of 2.3.

The relationships among the four measures depend on stand age, on species, and on ecological regime. The ESI to BGI correlation flips sign at 120 years (negative 0.57 in middle-age stands to positive 0.33 in old growth); species-level range from 0.028 in yellow-poplar to 0.492 in Douglas-fir. A national productivity ranking using a single measure absorbs all this structure into the response variable.

CSPI v2 ships at 30 meters across CONUS as a 3-component composite plus the four component layers as standalone citable products. Concept DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.20515034. Manuscript prepared for Forest Ecology and Management. Pipeline at github.com/holoros/fvs-conus. Funded by USDA NIFA McIntire-Stennis.

## LinkedIn variant B (medium length, broader research audience)

Forest site productivity is multi-dimensional. New CRSF analysis at 66,000 plus FIA plots: site index, biomass growth, asymptotic biomass, and net primary production carry overlapping but partially independent information. No measure can be substituted for any other without loss.

The cleanest empirical demonstration: a random forest predicting FIA's own seven-class site productivity classification reaches R squared of 0.808 from biomass growth alone, only 0.751 from site index alone. The Forest Service's classification has been quietly tracking biomass growth for decades.

Practical implication: choose your productivity measure by application. Height-growth-and-yield work uses site index; carbon stock projections use biomass growth; long-run carbon accounting uses asymptotic biomass; land surface modeling uses NPP. The CSPI v2 composite ranks general site quality without committing to one dimension.

Open data: Zenodo concept DOI 10.5281/zenodo.20515034.
