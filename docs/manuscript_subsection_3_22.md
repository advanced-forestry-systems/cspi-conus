## 3.22 External validation against repeat satellite productivity

*Draft subsection for integration into the v0.10 manuscript by manuscript-preparer. Plaintext math, no hyphens in prose. Numbers are from the 21 June 2026 repeat RS validation run, n = 38,978 unique plots.*

The cross prediction, classification, and stratification analyses above are computed within the FIA measurement system. A reviewer may reasonably ask whether the multi dimensional structure is an artifact of shared FIA lineage. We therefore tested the four productivity measures against an independent observation system: the MODIS MOD17A3HGF net primary productivity and gross primary productivity composites for 2017 to 2023, extracted at the FIA plot coordinates.

The biomass growth increment (BGI) tracks observed satellite productivity flux, while site index does not. BGI correlates with observed GPP at a pooled Pearson r = +0.74 and with observed NPP at +0.61 across 38,978 plots; the unified target site index (ESI) correlates negatively (r = -0.38 with NPP, -0.41 with GPP). Consistent with the scale dependence this paper emphasizes elsewhere, the pooled BGI value is partly a between region aggregation effect: within region correlations are more modest (West r = +0.46, East +0.11 for GPP) and vary with latitude from -0.03 in the subtropical Southeast to +0.80 at 37 to 43 N, with r(BGI, NPP) slightly negative in the East, a pattern consistent with documented MOD17 light use efficiency limitations in southeastern evergreen forests. The relationship is not an artifact of shared structure with site index: a partial correlation controlling for ESI leaves observed NPP tracking BGI at +0.53 and GPP at +0.68, while ESI contributes negatively. The divergence between BGI and ESI seen in the FIA internal analyses is therefore reproduced in an independent satellite measurement chain. The asymptotic biomass measure (Asym) correlates negatively with the interannual coefficient of variation of NPP (r = -0.39 pooled), consistent with high biomass capacity sites being more temporally stable in their productivity.

The same structure appears in Sentinel-2 optical metrics. Lamb et al. (2020) identified the Sentinel-2 red-edge position (S2REP) as the most important remote sensing metric for site productivity in the Acadian region and used Sentinel-2 to improve BGI. At the CONUS plots, BGI correlates with S2REP at r = +0.70 and with the chlorophyll index red-edge at +0.76, while ESI correlates negatively with every optical index (r = -0.40 to -0.48). Adding S2REP to BGI raises the adjusted R^2 for predicting observed NPP from 0.37 to 0.44, generalizing the iBGI concept of Lamb et al. (2020), although part of that gain reflects shared optical lineage between S2REP and the MODIS NPP target rather than fully independent information.

The MIAMI climatology NPP layer, included as a component in the CSPI v2 composite, does not track observed NPP (Pearson r = -0.03). This confirms that the MIAMI surface behaves as a climate potential layer rather than an observation of realized productivity, and motivates the planned replacement of the MIAMI layer with the MOD17A3HGF observed NPP in a subsequent release.

Observed canopy height carries little site productivity signal on its own. At 29,089 forested plots, LANDFIRE 2023 canopy height is predicted by ESI at adjusted R^2 = 0.004, by BGI at 0.112, by Asym at 0.023, and by all three jointly at only 0.165. A single epoch canopy height is governed by stand age, disturbance history, and structure rather than by site productivity, which is why absolute height is nearly uninformative about the productivity measures. This result frames the productivity signal as residing in the rate of canopy height change rather than in the height itself, and points toward repeat observation of biomass increment (for example annual NPP trend or the ESA CCI Biomass aboveground biomass increment) as the appropriate independent validator for a future prediction focused release.
